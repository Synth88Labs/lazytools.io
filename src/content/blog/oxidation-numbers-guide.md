**Oxidation numbers look intimidating but follow a short priority list ‚ÄĒ and the last unknown always
falls out by balancing.** Fix the elements that have rules (group 1 is +1, oxygen is ‚ąí2, and so on), then
solve the one that's left so everything sums to the overall charge. For KMnO‚āĄ that instantly gives
**Mn = +7**.

<aside class="key-takeaways">

**Key takeaways**

- **Free element = 0**; a **monatomic ion = its charge**.
- **Group 1 = +1**, **group 2 = +2**, **fluorine = ‚ąí1**.
- **Hydrogen = +1** (usually) and **oxygen = ‚ąí2** (usually).
- **The sum equals the overall charge** ‚ÄĒ 0 for a neutral compound; use this to solve the last element.
- **Exceptions:** peroxides (O = ‚ąí1), metal hydrides (H = ‚ąí1), OF‚āā (O = +2).

</aside>

The rules are a **priority list** ‚ÄĒ when two could apply, the higher one wins:

1. **A free element is 0** (e.g. O‚āā, Na metal, S‚āą).
2. **A monatomic ion equals its charge** (Na‚Āļ is +1, Cl‚ĀĽ is ‚ąí1).
3. **Group 1 metals are +1; group 2 metals are +2.**
4. **Fluorine is always ‚ąí1.**
5. **Hydrogen is +1** ‚ÄĒ except in metal hydrides, where it's ‚ąí1.
6. **Oxygen is ‚ąí2** ‚ÄĒ except in peroxides (‚ąí1) and with fluorine (+2).
7. **The oxidation numbers sum to the overall charge** (0 for a neutral compound; the ion's charge otherwise).

## The trick: solve the last one by balancing

Rule 7 is the workhorse. Once every element with a rule is fixed, the remaining element is whatever makes
the sum come out right. Take **KMnO‚āĄ**:

- K (group 1) = **+1**
- O = **‚ąí2**, and there are four of them ‚Üí **‚ąí8**
- Everything must sum to 0 (neutral compound): `+1 + Mn + (‚ąí8) = 0`
- So **Mn = +7**.

## Mind the exceptions

Three cases break the default rules:

- **Peroxides** ‚ÄĒ oxygen is **‚ąí1** (as in hydrogen peroxide, H‚āāO‚āā).
- **Metal hydrides** ‚ÄĒ hydrogen is **‚ąí1** (as in NaH, CaH‚āā).
- **Oxygen difluoride, OF‚āā** ‚ÄĒ oxygen is **+2**, because fluorine is more electronegative.

When you spot a peroxide or a metal hydride, override the default before balancing.

## Why bother?

Oxidation numbers are how chemists **track electron transfer**. If an element's oxidation number goes up,
it was **oxidised** (lost electrons); if it goes down, it was **reduced** (gained electrons). That's the
foundation of balancing redox reactions ‚ÄĒ the same reactions the
[equation balancer](/chemistry/chemical-equation-balancer/) handles ‚ÄĒ and of electrochemistry.
