**Your BMR is the calories you'd burn lying in bed all day; your TDEE is that number multiplied by
how much you actually move — and TDEE is the one that decides whether you lose, hold, or gain
weight.** Get your BMR from the Mifflin–St Jeor equation, multiply by an activity factor, then eat
below it to lose or above it to gain. Run your own numbers in the
[TDEE & calorie calculator](/calc/tdee-calculator/) — everything below is the reasoning behind it.

## Step 1 — BMR from Mifflin–St Jeor

Basal metabolic rate is what your body spends just staying alive: keeping your heart beating, your
lungs working, your cells repairing. It's the biggest slice of most people's daily burn — usually
60–70% of the total. The **Mifflin–St Jeor equation**, published in 1990, is the one dietitians reach
for because it tracks measured metabolism more closely than the older Harris–Benedict:

- **Men:** BMR = 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) − 5 × age + 5
- **Women:** BMR = 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) − 5 × age − 161

Work it for a 30-year-old man, 75 kg, 175 cm: 750 + 1093.75 − 150 + 5 = **1,699 calories a day** at
complete rest. If you think in pounds and inches, the [calculator](/calc/tdee-calculator/) converts
(1 lb = 0.4536 kg, 1 in = 2.54 cm) before applying the formula, so you don't have to.

## Step 2 — scale it to your life with an activity factor

Almost nobody spends the day at rest, so BMR alone understates your needs. Multiply by an **activity
factor** to get TDEE:

| Activity level | Factor | Who it's for |
|---|---|---|
| Sedentary | 1.2 | Desk job, little or no exercise |
| Light | 1.375 | Light exercise 1–3 days/week |
| Moderate | 1.55 | Moderate exercise 3–5 days/week |
| Active | 1.725 | Hard exercise 6–7 days/week |
| Very active | 1.9 | Physical job or twice-daily training |

Our example man at the **moderate** factor: 1,699 × 1.55 ≈ **2,633 calories a day**. That's his TDEE —
the calories that keep his weight steady.

The single most common mistake here is over-rating yourself. A gym session three times a week on top
of a desk job is *light-to-moderate*, not *active*. When you're between two levels, choose the lower
one: it's easier to add food later than to explain why a too-generous estimate stalled your progress.

## Step 3 — turn TDEE into a goal

TDEE is your maintenance line. To change weight, create a gap:

- **To lose:** eat below TDEE. A pound of body fat stores roughly **3,500 calories**, so a **500
  kcal/day** deficit ≈ 1 lb (0.45 kg) a week; 250/day ≈ half a pound. In our example, ~2,133 kcal/day
  targets a half-kilo weekly loss.
- **To maintain:** eat at your TDEE.
- **To gain:** eat above it. A 500/day surplus supports roughly a pound a week, though for muscle gain
  a smaller 250–300 surplus wastes less as fat.

Two guardrails. Don't drop below your **BMR** for extended periods — that's the floor your organs
need. And bigger deficits aren't better: past ~1,000/day you start burning muscle and the plan gets
hard to keep. Once you know your target calories, split them into protein, carbs and fat with the
[macro calculator](/calc/macro-calculator/), and sanity-check your goal weight against a healthy range
with the [ideal weight calculator](/calc/ideal-weight-calculator/).

## Why the number is an estimate, not a verdict

Every formula on this page is a population average fitted to your height, weight, age and sex. It
can't see your muscle mass (metabolically pricier than fat), your thyroid, or your **NEAT** — the
calories you burn fidgeting, pacing and generally not sitting still, which varies by hundreds of
calories between people. Realistically your true TDEE sits within about **±10–15%** of the estimate.

So use it the way a coach would: eat at the target for two to three weeks, watch the *trend* on the
scale (not any single day, which is mostly water), and adjust by 100–200 calories if reality and the
math disagree. The formula gives you a smart starting point; your own results are the calibration.
This is general information, not medical advice — check with a professional before big changes,
especially if you have a health condition.

## Quick summary

BMR is what you burn at rest (Mifflin–St Jeor: 10×kg + 6.25×cm − 5×age ± sex constant). TDEE is BMR
times an activity factor from 1.2 to 1.9, and it's the number that governs your weight: eat ~500 below
it to lose about a pound a week, at it to maintain, above it to gain. Pick your activity level
honestly, never live below your BMR, and treat the result as a two-week experiment to refine. Get your
own figures — privately, in your browser — from the
[TDEE & calorie calculator](/calc/tdee-calculator/), then plan the plate with the
[macro calculator](/calc/macro-calculator/).
