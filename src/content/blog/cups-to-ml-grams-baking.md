**One US cup is 236.59 mL — but "a cup" is not one thing worldwide.** Australia and New Zealand bake
with a 250 mL metric cup, Japan with a 200 mL cup, and older UK recipes with a 284 mL breakfast cup.
That spread — up to 20% between systems — is behind a large share of "the recipe failed" stories.
Convert any amount exactly with the [cups to mL converter](/units/cups-to-ml/), which runs entirely in
your browser.

## The cup systems of the world

| Cup standard | mL | Where you'll meet it |
|---|---|---|
| US customary cup | 236.59 | American recipes (the default online) |
| US "legal" cup | 240 | US nutrition labels |
| Metric cup | 250 | Australia, NZ, Canada (modern), India |
| Japanese cup | 200 | Japanese recipes, rice cookers* |
| UK breakfast cup (legacy) | 284 | pre-1970s British cookbooks |

*Rice-cooker "cups" are their own thing: the gō, 180 mL. If your rice cooker came with a small plastic
cup, that's it.

The dangerous pair is US 236.59 vs metric 250 — close enough to seem interchangeable, different enough
(5.7%) to change a batter across several cups. When a recipe's origin is unclear, assume US if it's
from a US site; [convert exactly](/units/cups-to-ml/) rather than eyeballing.

## Cups to grams: the density problem

A cup is a *volume*; a gram is a *weight* — so "1 cup in grams" depends entirely on what's in the cup.
The reference table for common baking ingredients (US cup, spooned and leveled):

| Ingredient | 1 US cup ≈ grams | 1 US cup ≈ oz |
|---|---|---|
| All-purpose flour | 120 g | 4.2 oz |
| Granulated sugar | 200 g | 7.1 oz |
| Brown sugar (packed) | 220 g | 7.8 oz |
| Powdered sugar | 120 g | 4.2 oz |
| Butter | 227 g | 8.0 oz (2 sticks) |
| Milk / water | 237–240 g | 8.4 oz |
| Cocoa powder | 85 g | 3.0 oz |
| Rolled oats | 90 g | 3.2 oz |
| Honey / syrup | 340 g | 12.0 oz |
| Rice (uncooked) | 185 g | 6.5 oz |

Water is the anchor: 1 mL weighs 1 g, so a cup of water is ~237 g. Everything lighter (flour, cocoa)
or heavier (honey) departs from that. For weight conversions between metric and US units, the
[grams to ounces converter](/units/g-to-oz/) covers the exact math.

<p><span class="co-label">⚠️ The flour trap</span> "1 cup flour" spans 120–150 g depending on whether you spoon it in gently or scoop and pack it. That 25% swing is the single most common cause of dense cakes and dry cookies. Fluff, spoon, level — or better, weigh.</p>

## Spoons: the small print

- **1 US tablespoon = 14.79 mL** — functionally the metric 15 mL tablespoon
- **3 teaspoons = 1 tablespoon; 16 tablespoons = 1 cup**
- **1 US teaspoon = 4.93 mL** ≈ metric 5 mL
- **Australian tablespoon = 20 mL** (four teaspoons) — the classic trans-Tasman recipe pitfall
- UK recipes' "dessertspoon" = 10 mL, halfway between teaspoon and tablespoon

Convert any spoon amount with [tbsp to mL](/units/tbsp-to-ml/) or [tsp to mL](/units/tsp-to-ml/).

## Scaling recipes without wrecking them

Doubling works cleanly for volumes and weights, with three caveats bakers learn the hard way:

1. **Leaveners don't scale linearly past 2×** — baking powder/soda often need only ~1.5× when tripling,
   or bakes taste metallic and rise unevenly.
2. **Pan area, not volume, drives baking time.** A doubled batter in the same pan bakes far longer and
   drier; use two pans or a pan with ~2× the base area.
3. **Salt scales to taste, not math** — scale to ~80% and adjust.

## Why weight beats volume (and always has)

Professional kitchens and serious home bakers work in grams for three reasons: **accuracy** (a scale
doesn't care about compaction, humidity or scooping style), **speed** (tare the bowl, pour to the
number — no washing six measuring cups), and **scaling** (percentages of flour weight — "baker's
percentages" — make any batch size trivial). A 20-dollar kitchen scale removes the entire class of
cup-conversion errors this article exists to fix. Until then, the
[converters](/units/cups-to-ml/) keep the math exact even when the cups aren't.
