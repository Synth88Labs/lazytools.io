**If your dog has eaten chocolate, call your vet or a pet poison helpline now — don't wait for symptoms.** With that said, here's the honest answer to "how much is too much": it depends on two things — the *type* of chocolate (how much theobromine it contains) and your dog's *body weight*. A few squares of milk chocolate might barely register for a Labrador but seriously harm a Chihuahua, and the same weight of dark or baking chocolate is several times more dangerous.

<aside class="key-takeaways">

**Key takeaways**

- **Theobromine is the poison** — a caffeine-like compound dogs clear very slowly (half-life ~17.5 hours).
- **Dose is what matters:** milligrams of theobromine per kilogram of the dog's body weight.
- **Vet thresholds:** signs from about **20 mg/kg**, serious heart/nerve effects **40–60 mg/kg**, potentially fatal **over ~100 mg/kg**.
- **Darker = deadlier:** baking chocolate and cocoa powder have the most; milk chocolate far less; white chocolate almost none.
- **When in doubt, call** — US ASPCA Animal Poison Control **888-426-4435**, UK Animal PoisonLine **01202 509000**.

</aside>

## Why chocolate is poisonous to dogs

Chocolate contains **theobromine**, a methylxanthine chemically related to caffeine (which chocolate also contains in smaller amounts). Humans metabolise theobromine quickly, but dogs break it down far more slowly — its half-life in a dog is roughly **17.5 hours**, so it builds up and keeps acting for a day or more. At high enough doses it overstimulates the heart and nervous system.

The key insight is that toxicity is a **dose per body weight** problem, measured in milligrams of theobromine per kilogram. That's why a small dog and a large dog eating the *same* bar face very different risks.

## The risk thresholds vets use

Published veterinary references (the Merck Veterinary Manual and pet poison-control services) band the risk roughly like this, per kilogram of the dog's weight:

| Theobromine dose | What tends to happen |
| --- | --- |
| **Under 20 mg/kg** | Often no signs, or mild stomach upset |
| **20–40 mg/kg** | Vomiting, diarrhoea, restlessness, extreme thirst |
| **40–60 mg/kg** | Racing/irregular heartbeat, tremors, agitation |
| **60–100 mg/kg** | Seizures, dangerous heart rhythms |
| **Over ~100 mg/kg** | Potentially fatal |

These are guidelines, not guarantees — individual dogs vary, and older dogs or those with heart conditions are more vulnerable.

## How much theobromine is in each chocolate type

Content varies by product, but typical mid-range figures are:

| Chocolate type | Theobromine (approx.) |
| --- | --- |
| White chocolate | ~0.009 mg/g (negligible) |
| Milk chocolate | ~2.1 mg/g |
| Dark / semisweet | ~5.4 mg/g |
| Baking (unsweetened) | ~14 mg/g |
| Cocoa powder | ~26 mg/g |

So gram for gram, **baking chocolate is roughly 7× as dangerous as milk chocolate**, and cocoa powder more still.

## A worked example

Say a **10 kg** dog eats **50 g of milk chocolate** (about half a large bar):

- Theobromine ≈ 2.1 mg/g × 50 g = **≈ 103 mg**
- Dose ≈ 103 mg ÷ 10 kg = **≈ 10 mg/kg** → below the 20 mg/kg threshold, likely mild.

Now swap in **50 g of dark chocolate**:

- Theobromine ≈ 5.4 mg/g × 50 g = **≈ 268 mg**
- Dose ≈ 268 ÷ 10 = **≈ 27 mg/kg** → into the "expect vomiting and restlessness" band.

## What to do if your dog ate chocolate

1. **Don't wait for symptoms** — theobromine is slow, so signs can be hours away. Act now.
2. **Work out what and how much:** the type of chocolate, roughly how much, and your dog's weight. Keep the wrapper.
3. **Call for advice:** your vet, or a pet poison line — US ASPCA Animal Poison Control **888-426-4435**, UK Animal PoisonLine **01202 509000**.
4. **Follow their instructions.** Don't try to make your dog vomit unless a professional tells you to.
5. **Watch for signs** over the next 24–72 hours: vomiting, diarrhoea, hyperactivity, a pounding heart, tremors or seizures.

The good news: with prompt treatment, most dogs recover well. The danger comes from waiting.

## Frequently asked questions

### How much chocolate can kill a dog?
Roughly, a theobromine dose over about 100 mg/kg of body weight can be fatal. That's around 50 g of baking chocolate for a 10 kg dog, or a much larger amount of milk chocolate. But dogs can become seriously ill well below the lethal dose, so any chocolate ingestion warrants a call to your vet.

### Is a small amount of chocolate OK for dogs?
There's no amount of chocolate that's *good* for a dog. A tiny lick of milk chocolate is unlikely to harm a large dog, but the same amount is riskier for a small one, and darker chocolate raises the stakes sharply. It's safest to keep all chocolate out of reach.

### Which chocolate is most dangerous?
Baking (unsweetened) chocolate and cocoa powder contain the most theobromine, followed by dark/semisweet, then milk chocolate. White chocolate has almost none, so theobromine poisoning is unlikely — though its fat and sugar can still cause stomach upset or pancreatitis.

### How long after eating chocolate will a dog show symptoms?
Signs typically appear within 6–12 hours and can last 24–72 hours because dogs clear theobromine so slowly. This delay is exactly why you should act immediately rather than waiting to see if your dog "seems fine."

### My dog ate white chocolate — should I worry about theobromine?
Theobromine poisoning is unlikely from white chocolate because it contains almost none. However, the high fat and sugar can trigger vomiting, diarrhoea or pancreatitis, so still monitor your dog and call your vet if it ate a large amount or shows signs of illness.

### Can dogs recover from chocolate poisoning?
Yes — with prompt veterinary care, most dogs recover fully. Treatment may include inducing vomiting, activated charcoal, IV fluids and heart monitoring. Outcomes are best when owners act quickly rather than waiting for symptoms to worsen.
