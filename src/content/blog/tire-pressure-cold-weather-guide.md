**Tires lose about 1 PSI for every 10°F (roughly 2% of pressure per 10°F) that the ambient temperature falls.** So a 30°F overnight drop costs about 3 PSI — enough that a tire which read correctly yesterday afternoon can set off the dashboard warning this morning, with nothing wrong and no puncture. It's physics, not a fault. The fix is to add air back to the door-placard spec, **not** to overinflate in anticipation of winter.

- **≈1 PSI lost per 10°F drop** (about 2% of total pressure).
- **TPMS warns at roughly 25% below** the placard pressure — a low-but-legal tire may not trigger it.
- **"Cold pressure" means cold tires**, not cold weather: parked 3+ hours, or first thing in the morning.

- Use the **door-jamb placard**, never the number moulded on the tire sidewall (that's a maximum).
- Check monthly; weekly during hard cold snaps and before long trips.

## The physics in one line

Air pressure in a sealed, fixed volume rises and falls with temperature. When air cools, its molecules move more slowly and exert less force on the tire's inner surface — so the gauge reads lower. Nothing has escaped; the same air is simply pushing less hard.

The working rule of thumb is **1 PSI per 10°F**, or about **2% of the tire's pressure per 10°F**. On a typical 35 PSI passenger tire those are nearly the same number, which is why the simpler version has stuck.

| Temperature drop | Pressure lost (from 35 PSI) | Resulting pressure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 10°F | ~1 PSI | ~34 PSI |
| 20°F | ~2 PSI | ~33 PSI |
| 30°F | ~3 PSI | ~32 PSI |
| 50°F | ~5 PSI | ~30 PSI |

## Why the warning light picks winter

A Tire Pressure Monitoring System triggers its warning at roughly **25% below** the vehicle's recommended pressure. On a 35 PSI placard that's about 26 PSI.

Now stack the effects. A tire sitting at 31 PSI in autumn — already a little low, not low enough to warn — meets a 40°F cold snap and drops to about 27 PSI. One more cold night and the light comes on. Nothing failed; a slightly soft tire simply crossed the threshold as the season turned.

Two consequences worth internalising:

1. **The light coming on in the first hard cold is normal.** Check and inflate before assuming a puncture.
2. **No light does not mean correct pressure.** A 25% margin is a lot. A tire can be meaningfully underinflated — costing fuel economy, tread life and wet grip — while the dashboard stays dark. That's why monthly manual checks still matter.

## What "cold tire pressure" really means

This trips up almost everyone. **"Cold" refers to the tire, not the weather.**

Driving flexes the tire and heats the air inside; pressure climbs as you go. Check a tire straight after a motorway run and you'll see a reading several PSI above its resting value — inflate to the placard number then, and you'll be **underinflated** once it cools.

Correct practice:

- Check **first thing in the morning**, or after the car has sat for **at least three hours**.
- If you must drive to a compressor, drive as short a distance as possible and accept the reading will be a little high — or better, inflate at home.
- Never bleed air out of hot tires to "correct" a high reading. The pressure rise is normal and the tire will be under-inflated once cool.

## Don't overinflate to pre-empt the cold

A tempting mistake: add a few extra PSI in autumn so the winter drop lands on target.

Don't. The placard pressure is the vehicle manufacturer's figure for that car's weight, handling and braking. Overinflation shrinks the contact patch toward the centre of the tread, which means:

- **Less grip**, particularly on wet or icy roads — exactly when you need it
- **Uneven wear** down the middle of the tread
- **A harsher ride** and more impact damage from potholes

Instead, check more often through the cold months and top up to the placard figure each time.

Equally, the number moulded into the **sidewall is a maximum**, not a recommendation — it's the pressure the tire is rated to hold, usually well above what your car should run. The correct figure lives on the **driver's door jamb placard** (sometimes the fuel-filler flap or glovebox).

## What underinflation actually costs

Beyond the warning light, running soft has real running costs. Underinflated tires flex more, which raises rolling resistance and **fuel consumption**, builds heat that accelerates wear, and wears the **shoulders** of the tread faster than the centre. Severe underinflation at speed is a genuine blowout risk because of that heat build-up.

The same physics runs in reverse in summer: a tire set correctly on a cold morning gains pressure through a hot afternoon. That's expected and designed for — which is exactly why the specification is a *cold* pressure.

## Frequently asked questions

### Why does my tire pressure light come on in cold weather?
Because air pressure falls with temperature — about 1 PSI per 10°F. A tire that was slightly low already can cross the TPMS warning threshold (roughly 25% below the recommended pressure) after an overnight cold snap, with no leak or puncture involved.

### How much pressure do tires lose in cold weather?
Roughly 1 PSI for every 10°F drop, or about 2% of the tire's pressure per 10°F. A 30°F drop costs about 3 PSI on a typical passenger tire.

### Should I inflate my tires more in winter?
No — inflate to the pressure on the door-jamb placard, measured when the tires are cold. Deliberately overinflating to offset the cold reduces your contact patch and grip on exactly the surfaces where you need it most, and wears the centre of the tread.

### When should I check tire pressure?
When the tires are cold: first thing in the morning, or after the vehicle has been parked at least three hours. Driving heats the air inside and inflates the reading, so a warm check will make an underinflated tire look fine.

### Where do I find the correct tire pressure?
On the placard in the driver's door jamb (sometimes the fuel-filler flap or glovebox). Do not use the number moulded on the tire sidewall — that's the tire's maximum rated pressure, not your vehicle's recommended one.

### Will the TPMS light go off by itself?
Often yes, once the tires warm up on a drive or the day heats up and pressure rises back above the threshold — but that doesn't mean the pressure is correct. Check the tires cold and inflate to spec. A light that returns repeatedly, or comes on with correct cold pressures, suggests a slow leak or a sensor fault.

### Does nitrogen stop pressure changing with temperature?
No. Nitrogen follows the same gas laws as air and changes pressure with temperature in essentially the same way. Its claimed benefits relate to slower permeation through the tire and less moisture, not immunity from the temperature effect — you still need to check pressures seasonally.
