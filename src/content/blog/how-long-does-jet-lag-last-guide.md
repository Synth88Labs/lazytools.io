**Left alone, jet lag takes about one day per time zone crossed flying west, and roughly 1.5 days per time zone flying east.** A six-zone westward trip resolves in about six days; the same six zones eastward can take eight to ten. The direction matters more than the distance — and correctly timed light exposure is the one intervention that reliably compresses it, to roughly two to three time zones of adjustment per day.

<aside class="key-takeaways">

**Key takeaways**

- **West ≈ 1 day per zone. East ≈ 1.5 days per zone.** Direction beats distance.
- East is harder because most people's body clock naturally runs **slightly longer than 24 hours** — so delaying (west) is easy, advancing (east) fights biology.
- **Light is the strongest lever.** Timed correctly it can shift the clock ~2–3 zones per day.
- **Flying east:** get morning light, avoid evening light. **Flying west:** avoid morning light, get evening light.
- Trips under ~3 days: consider **staying on home time** rather than adapting twice.
- This is general information, not medical advice — see a clinician about sleep medication or melatonin.

## Why east is harder than west

Your circadian clock isn't exactly 24 hours. For roughly three quarters of people it runs slightly *longer* than 24 hours, which the daily cycle of light resets each morning.

That small detail explains the whole east–west asymmetry:

- **Flying west lengthens your day.** You stay up later and sleep later — a **phase delay**. Your clock already drifts that direction, so you're working with your biology.
- **Flying east shortens your day.** You must fall asleep and wake earlier — a **phase advance**. That's the direction your clock resists.

Research on the brain's master clock (the suprachiasmatic nucleus) finds it responds more sluggishly to eastward shifts, needing meaningfully more recovery time. The effect is strong enough that a *shorter* eastward trip can take longer to recover from than a *longer* westward one — five zones east can outlast seven zones west.

## The baseline: how many days

Without any deliberate intervention, plan for:

| Zones crossed | Westward (≈1 day/zone) | Eastward (≈1.5 days/zone) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 3 | ~3 days | ~4–5 days |
| 5 | ~5 days | ~7–8 days |
| 6 | ~6 days | ~8–10 days |
| 8 | ~8 days | ~11–12 days |

These are rough averages — age, sleep quality before departure, and individual chronotype all shift them. The [jet lag calculator](/travel/jet-lag-calculator/) works out your zone count and an adjustment schedule for a specific route.

## Light: the one intervention that reliably works

Light is the dominant signal that sets your circadian clock, which makes *timed* light exposure — and just as importantly, timed light **avoidance** — the most powerful tool available. Used correctly it can compress adaptation to roughly **two to three time zones per day**, and combined with melatonin the clock can shift on the order of 3–4 hours daily.

The direction of travel flips the strategy completely:

| Direction | Seek light | Avoid light |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Eastward** (advance the clock) | **Morning** at destination | Evening |
| **Westward** (delay the clock) | **Evening** at destination | Morning |

Get this backwards and you actively push your clock the wrong way — which is why "just get sunlight" is incomplete advice. Flying east and sitting outside all evening will *delay* the clock you're trying to advance.

Practical version: **flying east**, seek bright morning light on arrival and wear sunglasses / stay dim in the late evening. **Flying west**, keep mornings dim and get outside in the late afternoon and evening.

## Shifting before you fly

You don't have to start on arrival. Shifting your sleep schedule by an hour a day in the days before departure — earlier for eastward trips, later for westward — means you land already partly adapted. Studies of pre-flight advancing (moving sleep earlier, paired with morning bright light) show the clock can be meaningfully shifted before an eastward flight, reducing the deficit on arrival.

Even two or three days of an hour's shift removes two or three days from the far end. Combine it with the [time zone converter](/travel/time-zone-converter/) so you're planning against destination time from the start.

## When *not* to adapt

For a trip of about **three days or less**, adapting is often the wrong goal. You'd shift your clock one way, then have to shift it back — two rounds of jet lag for a short stay.

For short trips, consider staying close to home time: schedule important meetings during hours that overlap your home-time daytime, keep your usual sleep window if the local schedule allows, and treat it like a night shift rather than a relocation.

## What actually helps (and what doesn't much)

**Helps:**
- Timed light exposure and avoidance — the strongest lever by far
- Shifting your schedule 1 hour/day before departure
- Switching your watch, phone and meal times to destination time on boarding
- Sleeping on the plane only if it's night at your destination

**Overrated:**
- "Beat jet lag" diets — evidence is thin compared with light timing
- Alcohol as a sleep aid — it fragments sleep and worsens next-day fatigue
- Forcing daytime naps longer than ~20–30 minutes, which erodes the night's sleep pressure you need

**Hydration** is worth doing for cabin-air comfort, but dehydration and jet lag are separate problems — drinking water doesn't move your circadian clock.

## Frequently asked questions

### How long does jet lag last?
Roughly one day per time zone crossed when flying west, and about 1.5 days per zone flying east. Six zones west is about six days; six zones east can be eight to ten. Timed light exposure can cut this to two or three zones of adjustment per day.

### Is jet lag worse going east or west?
East, for most people. About three quarters of us have a circadian clock that naturally runs slightly longer than 24 hours, so flying west (a later day, a phase delay) works with that drift, while flying east (an earlier day, a phase advance) fights it.

### How do I get over jet lag faster?
Time your light exposure to the direction you flew: after an eastward flight seek morning light and avoid evening light; after a westward flight avoid morning light and seek evening light. Shift your sleep an hour a day before departure, and switch to destination time as soon as you board.

### Does jet lag get worse with age?
Many people find recovery slower with age, as circadian rhythms tend to become less flexible and sleep more fragmented. The direction rule still holds — eastward remains the harder adjustment.

### Should I nap when I arrive?
Short naps of about 20–30 minutes can take the edge off without eating into your night sleep. Long afternoon naps reduce the sleep pressure you need to fall asleep at local bedtime, which prolongs the adjustment.

### Does melatonin help with jet lag?
Timed melatonin is commonly used alongside light to shift the clock, and combined they can move it several hours a day. Timing and dose matter and they vary by direction of travel, so it's worth discussing with a clinician or pharmacist rather than guessing — this article is general information, not medical advice.

### Is jet lag the same as travel fatigue?
No. Travel fatigue is the tiredness from the journey itself — dry cabin air, cramped seating, disrupted routine — and it resolves after a good night's sleep. Jet lag is a misalignment between your body clock and local time, and it persists for days until the clock resets. A north–south flight can cause travel fatigue with no jet lag at all.
