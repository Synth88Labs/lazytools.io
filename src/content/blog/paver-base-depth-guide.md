**A paver patio or walkway needs 4–6 inches of compacted gravel with 1 inch of bedding sand on top. A driveway needs 8–12 inches of gravel, because vehicle loads roughly double the requirement.** Almost every failed paver job — sinking, rutting, spreading edges — traces back to one of three things: not enough base, base compacted all at once instead of in thin lifts, or too much sand used to hide an uneven base.

<aside class="key-takeaways">

**Key takeaways**

- **Patios/walkways: 4–6″ compacted gravel.** **Driveways: 8–12″.**
- **Bedding sand: 1 inch, screeded — never thicker.** Sand is a levelling layer, not a base.
- **Compact in 2–3″ lifts.** A plate compactor can't densify a 6″ dump in one pass.
- **Slope 1/4″ per foot (2%)** away from the house — build it into the base, not the pavers.
- **Edge restraint is not optional.** Without it the field spreads and joints open.
- Total dig = pavers + 1″ sand + base depth. Budget the spoil — soil bulks up ~20–30%.

</aside>

## How deep, by what it carries

| Use | Compacted gravel base | Bedding sand |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Walkway / path | 4″ | 1″ |
| Patio | 4–6″ | 1″ |
| Driveway (cars) | 8″ | 1″ |
| Driveway (heavy vehicles) | 10–12″ | 1″ |

Go deeper in two situations: **poor-draining clay subgrade**, and **cold climates with hard frost**, where a deeper free-draining base reduces frost heave. If your soil stays wet and soft, adding base depth is cheaper than rebuilding.

Work out quantities with the [paver calculator](/home/paver-calculator/) and the [gravel calculator](/home/gravel-calculator/).

## Total excavation depth

Dig depth = **paver thickness + 1″ sand + base depth**.

A typical patio with 2⅜″ pavers over a 6″ base needs about **9½ inches** of excavation. Add a little for the string line and get the depth right before you start — deepening a partly built patio is miserable work.

Remember that excavated soil **swells 20–30%** once loose, so the pile is bigger than the hole. The [excavation calculator](/home/excavation-calculator/) accounts for that when you're sizing a skip or trailer.

## Compact in lifts — this is the step people skip

A plate compactor only densifies material a few inches down. Dump 6 inches of gravel and run the compactor over it and you'll get a firm crust over loose stone — which consolidates unevenly under load, months later, as dips in your finished patio.

Correct method:

1. **Compact the subgrade first**, before any gravel goes in.
2. Add gravel in **2–3 inch lifts**.
3. **Compact each lift** before adding the next. Multiple passes in different directions.
4. Lightly dampening the gravel helps it compact — it should be moist, not muddy.

Use a **well-graded crushed aggregate** (a mix of stone sizes down to fines, often sold as road base, crusher run or MOT Type 1). The fines lock the larger stone together. Uniform, single-size clean gravel does *not* compact into a rigid base — it stays loose.

## Why the sand layer must stay at 1 inch

The bedding layer is for **setting pavers to a consistent level**, nothing more. Its thickness is deliberately small because sand doesn't compact into a stable structure — it shifts under load.

The classic DIY mistake is using extra sand to smooth out a base that wasn't levelled properly. It looks fine on day one, then the thick spots consolidate at a different rate from the thin ones and the surface goes wavy within a season.

Screed the sand to a uniform 1 inch using pipe rails, **don't compact it before laying**, and set pavers directly onto it. The pavers are compacted into the sand at the end, which locks everything together.

Use **coarse, sharp concrete sand** — not fine play sand or builder's sand, which holds water and shifts.

## Slope: build it into the base

Water must run off, and away from the house. The standard is **1/4 inch per foot** — a 2% fall. Over a 12-foot patio, that's a 3-inch drop across its width.

Crucially, **build the slope into the excavation and the base**, so the gravel and sand layers stay uniform in thickness. Trying to create fall by varying the sand depth reintroduces the thick-sand problem above.

## Edge restraint: the omission that spreads a patio

Pavers rely on interlock. Without something holding the perimeter, the outer course pushes outward under traffic, joints open, sand escapes, and the field creeps — usually starting within a year or two.

Install plastic or metal **edge restraint** spiked into the compacted base (not into the sand), or a concrete haunch, around every unsupported edge. It's a small line item with an outsized effect on how long the job lasts.

## The finishing step

After laying, sweep **joint sand** across the surface and run the plate compactor over the pavers (with a protective mat) to vibrate sand into the joints and seat the pavers into the bedding layer. Repeat the sweep-and-compact cycle until the joints stay full. **Polymeric sand** hardens slightly when wetted and resists washout and weeds better than plain sand — follow its instructions closely, since it must be properly cleaned off the paver faces before wetting.

## Frequently asked questions

### How deep should a paver base be?
4–6 inches of compacted gravel for patios and walkways, plus 1 inch of bedding sand. Driveways need 8–12 inches of gravel because of vehicle loads. Go deeper on clay or poor-draining soil, and in hard-frost climates.

### Can I lay pavers on sand only?
Only for the most temporary, light-duty work. Sand doesn't provide structural support — without a compacted gravel base beneath it, pavers sink and rut under load and after rain. The sand's job is levelling, not bearing.

### How thick should the sand layer be under pavers?
One inch, screeded uniformly. Thicker sand is the most common cause of a wavy patio, because deep and shallow areas consolidate at different rates. Never use extra sand to compensate for an uneven base.

### What kind of gravel is best for a paver base?
A well-graded crushed aggregate containing a range of sizes down to fines — sold as road base, crusher run, or MOT Type 1. The fines lock the larger stone together when compacted. Uniform clean gravel with no fines won't form a rigid base.

### Do I really need edge restraint?
Yes. Without it the outer pavers gradually push outward, joints open, bedding sand escapes and the whole field spreads. Spike restraints into the compacted base, not into the sand.

### How much slope does a paver patio need?
About 1/4 inch per foot (2%), falling away from the building. Build the slope into the excavation and base layers so the gravel and sand stay uniform thickness throughout.

### Why is my paver patio sinking?
Usually insufficient base depth, base compacted in one thick layer instead of 2–3 inch lifts, an uncompacted subgrade, or too much bedding sand. Localised dips often mean a soft spot in the subgrade that was never compacted before the gravel went down.
