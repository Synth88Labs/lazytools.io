**"How many grams in a cup?" has no single answer — and that's the whole point.** A cup measures *volume* (how much space something fills), but grams measure *weight*. How much a cup weighs depends entirely on the ingredient's density: one US cup holds about **120 g of flour, 198 g of sugar, 226 g of butter, and 336 g of honey**. Any converter that gives you one fixed "grams per cup" number is wrong for almost everything.

<aside class="key-takeaways">

**Key takeaways**

- **A cup is volume; grams are weight** — the conversion changes with every ingredient.
- **One US cup:** flour ≈ 120 g, granulated sugar ≈ 198 g, butter ≈ 226 g, water ≈ 227 g, honey ≈ 336 g.
- **Denser, tightly-packing ingredients weigh more** per cup (sugar and honey beat flour).
- **Weigh, don't scoop** — flour especially compresses, so a "cup" can vary 20%+.
- **US cup = 236.6 mL, metric cup = 250 mL** — about 6% apart.

</aside>

## Volume vs weight — the core of it

When a recipe says "1 cup of flour," it's telling you to fill a cup-shaped container. But flour is light and full of air, while sugar is dense and packs tightly, and honey is a heavy liquid. Fill the same cup with each and you get very different weights. That's why there's no universal conversion — the number of grams in a cup is a property of the *ingredient*, not of the cup.

## The weights that matter most

Using King Arthur Baking's Ingredient Weight Chart (the US baking standard), per **US cup** (236.6 mL):

| Ingredient | Grams per cup |
|---|---|
| All-purpose / bread / cake flour | 120 g |
| Whole wheat flour | 113 g |
| Granulated (white) sugar | 198 g |
| Brown sugar (packed) | 213 g |
| Confectioners' (powdered) sugar | 113 g |
| Butter | 226 g |
| Water / milk | 227 g |
| Honey | 336 g |
| Cocoa powder | 84 g |

Notice how flour and powdered sugar land near 113–120 g (light, airy), while granulated sugar, butter and water cluster around 200–227 g, and honey — a dense liquid — tops 330 g. Same cup, wildly different weights.

## Why weighing beats scooping

Flour is the troublemaker. Scoop it straight from the bag and you compress it; a "cup" measured that way can weigh 140 g or more instead of 120 g — over 15% extra, enough to make a cake dry or a cookie tough. Spoon it into the cup and level it and you get closer to 120 g, but it's still inconsistent from person to person.

A kitchen scale removes all of that: 120 g of flour is 120 g every time, regardless of how packed it is. It's faster, more accurate, and means one fewer measuring cup to wash. This is why nearly every professional recipe — and a growing number of home baking books — lists ingredients by weight.

## The cup that isn't a cup

One more trap: not all cups are equal. The **US customary cup is 236.6 mL**, but the **metric cup** used in Australia, New Zealand and much of Europe is **250 mL** — about 6% larger. Older UK recipes sometimes used a different cup again. Gram-per-cup charts (including the one above) almost always mean the US cup, so if you're measuring with a 250 mL metric cup, you'll scoop slightly more. For the amounts in baking, that 6% can be the difference between success and a flop — another reason to switch to grams.
