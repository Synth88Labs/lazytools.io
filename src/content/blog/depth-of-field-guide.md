**That gorgeous blurred background — the one that makes a subject pop — is a shallow depth of field, and it's controlled by just four things.** Master them and you can dial in exactly how much of a photo is sharp: a paper-thin slice for a dreamy portrait, or everything from the flowers at your feet to the distant peaks.

<aside class="key-takeaways">

**Key takeaways**

- **Depth of field** = the zone that looks sharp. Shallow = blurry background.
- **Four controls:** aperture, focal length, subject distance, sensor size.
- **Blurry background:** wide aperture (low f-number), long lens, get close, big sensor.
- **Everything sharp:** small aperture (f/8–f/16), wide lens, step back, focus at the hyperfocal distance.
- Phones blur *in software* because their tiny sensors have huge depth of field.

</aside>

## 1. Aperture — the big one

The aperture is the adjustable opening in the lens, measured in f-numbers. It's the most direct control: a **wide aperture (a low f-number like f/1.8)** gives a **shallow** depth of field and a soft background; a **small aperture (a high f-number like f/16)** keeps far more in focus. Confusingly, smaller f-numbers mean bigger openings — f/1.8 is a wide aperture, f/16 is a narrow one. Start here: to blur a background, open up.

## 2. Focal length — longer blurs more

For the same aperture and subject size in the frame, a **longer lens gives a shallower depth of field**. An 85mm portrait lens throws a background far more out of focus than a 24mm wide-angle at the same f-stop. It's a big reason portrait photographers reach for 50mm, 85mm and 135mm lenses.

## 3. Subject distance — get close

**The closer you focus, the shallower the depth of field.** Move in tight on your subject and the background dissolves; back away and more comes into focus. This is why macro photos have razor-thin focus — at a few centimetres, the depth of field can be millimetres. It also means *distance between the subject and the background* matters: the farther the background, the blurrier it renders.

## 4. Sensor size — bigger blurs more

For the **same framing**, a **larger sensor gives a shallower depth of field**. A full-frame camera blurs backgrounds far more easily than a phone, because to get the same field of view a phone uses a very short lens — and short lenses have enormous depth of field. That's the whole reason your **phone fakes the blur** with a software "portrait mode" instead of doing it optically. ## The two recipes

Put the four together and you get a simple recipe for either extreme:

- **For a blurry background:** wide aperture (low f-number) · long lens · get close · big sensor · lots of distance to the background.
- **For everything sharp** (landscapes): small aperture (f/8–f/16) · wide lens · step back · and focus at the **hyperfocal distance** rather than the horizon, to squeeze out the maximum front-to-back sharpness.
