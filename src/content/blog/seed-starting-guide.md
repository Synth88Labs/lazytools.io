**Every vegetable-planting decision hangs on one date: your average last spring frost.** Start too early and tender seedlings get killed by a late cold snap or grow leggy waiting to go out. Start too late and slow crops like tomatoes never ripen. Get the frost date right and everything else is just counting weeks forward or backward from it.

<aside class="key-takeaways">

**Key takeaways**

- **The last frost date is the anchor** for the whole vegetable garden.
- **Count backward to start indoors:** onions ~10 wk, peppers ~8, tomatoes ~7, brassicas ~5.
- **Cool-season crops go out *before* frost:** peas, spinach, lettuce, kale, onions.
- **Warm-season crops go out *after* frost:** tomatoes, peppers, beans, squash, corn.
- These are **regional guidelines** — check your local frost date and the seed packet.

</aside>

## Why the last frost date runs everything

Plants split neatly into two camps. **Cool-season** crops (peas, lettuce, spinach, the cabbage family) tolerate light frost and actually prefer cool weather — they can go outside *weeks before* the last frost. **Warm-season** crops (tomatoes, peppers, beans, squash, corn) are killed or stunted by cold, so they wait until *after* it. Because that one date sorts everything, seed packets and planting guides express timing as "weeks before/after last frost" rather than fixed calendar dates — the same offset works whether you garden in a warm or cold region.

## Counting backward: when to start seeds indoors

Starting indoors gives slow crops a head start so they mature before autumn. Count back from the last frost:

| Crop | Start indoors | Move outdoors |
|---|---|---|
| Onions (from seed) | ~10 weeks before | ~4 weeks before frost |
| Peppers, eggplant | ~8 weeks before | ~2 weeks after frost |
| Tomatoes | ~6–8 weeks before | ~1 week after frost |
| Broccoli, cabbage, kale | ~5–6 weeks before | ~2–4 weeks before frost |
| Lettuce | ~4–5 weeks before | ~3 weeks before frost |
| Cucumber, squash | ~3–4 weeks before | ~1–2 weeks after frost |

Don't start *too* early: a tomato seedling started 12 weeks out becomes a root-bound, leggy plant stuck on a windowsill waiting for the weather. Matching the start date to the transplant date keeps plants stocky and healthy.

## Counting forward: sowing outdoors

Many crops skip the indoor stage entirely. **Cool-season** direct sowing happens *before* the frost — peas and spinach about 4–6 weeks before, lettuce a few weeks before. **Warm-season** direct sowing waits until *after* it — beans, corn, squash and cucumbers at or just after the last frost, when the soil has warmed. Root crops (carrots, beets) and legumes (peas, beans) are almost always direct-sown because they resent having their roots disturbed by transplanting.
