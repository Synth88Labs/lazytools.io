"Can I use Coca-Cola red?" The short answer is **yes, the color** — and it helps to see why. Two
different bodies of law are often confused here:

- **Copyright** protects creative works — a photograph, a piece of music, a written article. It has
  never protected a color; a hue is not an authored work.
- **Trademark** protects the identifiers that tell consumers *who* made something — names, logos,
  and occasionally a specific color used as branding. The U.S. Supreme Court confirmed in
  *[Qualitex v. Jacobson](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/514/159/)* (1995) that a color
  *can* function as a trademark — but only a specific color, for a specific product, that the public
  has come to associate with one source (Qualitex's green-gold dry-cleaning press pads). Famous
  examples: Tiffany's blue for jewelry packaging, UPS brown for delivery, T-Mobile magenta for
  telecoms.

Crucially, even a protected trade-dress color only stops uses that cause **consumer confusion in
that market**. Tiffany's blue doesn't stop you painting a wall, designing an unrelated app, or using
`#0abab5` in a data chart. What it stops is a rival jeweller packaging rings in that blue to trade on
Tiffany's reputation.

## The practical line: confusion, not color

Since the test is confusion, the everyday rule is simple:

**Normal, fine, done every day:**
- Building a mood board or palette study from brands you admire
- Matching a "Share on X" button to X's brand color, or an embed to a platform's UI
- Fan art, commentary, reviews, comparison articles
- Learning from how a brand's color system is constructed

**Where it turns into a trademark problem:**
- Designing a logo or product that *mimics* a brand's look to ride its recognition
- Implying a partnership or endorsement that doesn't exist
- Counterfeit packaging or trade-dress copying in the same market
- Using the brand's colors *and* name/logo to suggest your thing is their thing

Notice that in every "problem" case, the issue isn't the hex code — it's the impersonation. Having
`#e50914` in your CSS is not a Netflix problem; building "Netflox" with Netflix's red, wordmark and
layout is.

## Colors are a palette, and palettes change

Two last practical notes the [Brand Color Finder](/color/brand-colors/) is built around. First,
**a brand color is usually a system**: a primary plus secondaries and accents. Using only the
primary can look "off" precisely because the real identity carries supporting tones — which is why
each entry shows the whole palette, primary first.

Second, **brands rebrand**. Logo and palette refreshes happen constantly, so a documented hex is a
strong starting point, not a permanent fact. For inspiration and matching, the finder's values are
ideal. For contract, print, or anything where being *exactly* on-brand is contractual, pull the
brand's own current style guide as the source of truth — and if you spot a value that's gone stale,
the tool has a corrections link.
